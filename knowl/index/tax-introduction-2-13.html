Already we can see a difference between Euclidean geometry and taxicab geometry. In Euclidean geometry the path that is used to determine the distance between two points is along the line joining \(A \) and \(B \text{,}\) and it is unique. In taxicab geometry the path that is used to determine the distance between two points isn’t necessarily unique, as was seen in Figure 1.1. So, let’s call any path between two points \(A \) and \(B \) that is used to determine the taxicab distance between the points a taxicab path . Further, let’s call the set of all possible taxicab paths for the two points \(A \) and \(B \) the taxicab set for the points \(A \) and \(B \text{.}\)
